Keyboards can be tricky when it comes to intervals unlike guitars because guitars have frets and each fret represents a Semitone. If you skip a fret then it will be a Tone (or Whole Tone). Keyboards however has sequence of white and black keys, but not all white keys have black keys next to them like B and E.

Examples of Semitone intervals:

  • C to C#
  • C# to D
  • D to Eb
  • Tricky: E to F and B to C

Examples of Tone intervals:

  • C to D
  • D to E
  • C# to Eb
  • Tricky: Eb to F and E to F#

Example of Octave intervals:

  • C to higher C
  • D to higher D
  • E to lower E

Keyboard Runs

If you will play all notes in semitone intervals from middle C to higher C, you should play all white and black keys sequentially, not skipping any key.

But if you will play all notes in tone intervals from middle C to higher C, you would probably play all white keys because they sound “familiar”. But...

C - D - E - F - G - A - B - C – Wrong
C - D - E - F# - G# - Bb - C – Right

Major Chord

We learned that a major chord, or major triad, is built by adding the 3rd and 5th notes of the major scale to the root note. So, you can get the C major chord by playing C – E – G all across the keyboard.

Try to figure out and play other major chords.

Chord inversion is important while playing keyboard because not only it gives better melody to your chords but it helps you (or at least your right hand) to easily jump from one chord to another.

For example, C major chord has the following inversions:

Root position C – E – G
1st Inversion E – G – C
2nd Inversion G – C – E
