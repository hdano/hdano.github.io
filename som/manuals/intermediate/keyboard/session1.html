A musical keyboard is the set of adjacent depressible levers or keys on a musical instrument. They typically contain keys for playing the twelve notes of the Western musical scale, with a combination of larger, longer keys and smaller, shorter keys that repeats at the interval of an octave.

Depressing a key on the keyboard makes the instrument produce sounds, either by the following actions:

  • Making a hammer mechanically strike a string or tine (piano, electric piano, clavichord);
  • Plucking a string (harpsichord);
  • Opening a valve to let air flow through a pipe (pipe organ or accordion)
  • Striking a bell (carillon in a church tower).
  • Connecting to one or more circuits for electric and electronic keyboards (Hammond organ, digital piano, synthesizer, MIDI controller keyboard)

A pedal keyboard is a keyboard played with the feet, usually used with a pipe organ or theatre organ.

The number of keys in a keyboard varies depending on the instrument. A pedal keyboard only has 12 keys. A small modern MIDI keyboard can have 25 or 49 keys. Many home keyboards have standard 61 keys. And lastly, an acoustic piano has 88 keys.

The first electric keyboard instrument was the Denis d'or, which was built by Vaclav Prokop Dovis in 1753. It was incorporated with 700 strings temporarily electrified to make it sound louder. It was 4.9 ft long, 3 ft wide and 3.9 high, and only has 14 keys.

Electronic keyboards nowadays are so portable you can carry it in one hand, and has lots of keys and different sounds, and lots of features.

All-in-one. These keyboards have a keyboard, sound or synthesizer module, an amplifier and a set of speakers. Their keys are either unweighted or not touch-sensitive at all. And they are inexpensive and considered as home keyboards.

Professional Keyboards. Unlike the all-in-one’s, these keyboards only have a keyboard and better sound module. They are intended to be plugged in to an external keyboard amplifier. But their advantage is having professionally weighted keys, meaning you can play with more dynamics, especially in stage pianos.

MIDI Controller Keyboards. These are just controllers; they do not have their own sound. They have to connect to an external sound module or a computer that has sound producing software, which then connects to an external keyboard amplifier to produce sound.

A modern electronic keyboard can generate different sounds. Below is a list of some of the most common sounds keyboardists use during worship session:

  1. Piano sound. Piano has been a stable part of almost all genres of music – Pop, Classical, Rock, Ballad, Latin, etc. You can play piano as solo during worship or with the whole band. You can play it softly as background during altar calls, or you can play it hard during Rock praise songs.
  2. Electric Piano sound. This is a softer version of the piano sound, usually used in Jazz and RnB styles of songs.
  3. Pad sound. Unlike piano sound, this has long, smooth, ambient sound. Usually, you use this as a background for your main sound like piano or as a background for other band members like guitarists.
  4. Strings sound. Strings are basically collection of violin (and alike) sounds, usually used to add volume to the music. You usually add strings sound to louder part of the songs like chorus and bridge.
  5. Organ sound. Organ sound has been a huge part of early church music, and you can still use it today to add classic gospel effect on slow songs. You can play it smooth and silent like a pad sound or you can play it louder and crispier, with vibrato, during Rock songs.
  6. Lead sound. Lead sound is used when you are doing solo with your keyboard while the rest of the band, including your pad and piano, is backing you up. Almost any sound can be lead sound – piano, strings, organ, synthesizer or traditional lead instruments like flute and saxophone.
  7. Effects sound. Effects sound varies from sweeping bright pads to arpeggiated sequences, transforming shapes and phases. Just keep in mind that you are only adding right amount of flavors to the music, not destroying it and distracting the congregation.

White keys produce neutral notes, which are neither sharp nor flat. Black keys produce sharp (or flat) notes depending on what neutral note is on its left or right.

We learned from music theory class that a sharp note has a corresponding flat counterpart and those two are located on one black key for each octave.

For example, the first black key in the illustration is C# because it is at the right side of (or higher than) C white key. It is also Db because it is at the left side of (or lower than) D white key.

Lastly, the popular Middle C is just the note C at the center of your keyboard.

When reading and playing notations on keyboard, always use the middle C as reference. It is always located at the first ledger line below the Treble staff and above the Bass staff. The Treble staff corresponds to your right hand as the Bass staff to the left hand.
