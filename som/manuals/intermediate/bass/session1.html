Snare Drum - provides the strongest regular accents, played with sticks and usually plays the upbeats of a song. It produces its distinctive sound due to the snare wires fitted to the underside of the drum which buzz and vibrate against the bottom drum skin

Tom-tom(s) - (or just toms) are drums without snares, played also with sticks. They provide the bulk of most drum fills and solos. Smaller toms produce higher sound than bigger ones.

Floor Tom - kind of tom that stands on the floor usually in three legs. It produces the lowest sound among the toms.

Bass drum - (also known as kick drum) gives a regular but often varied foundation to the rhythm and played by pedal. It is the lowest sounding drum in a drum set and usually gives the downbeats along with basic patterns

Hi-hat cymbal - (or simply hi-hat) keeps the fine rhythm much of the time, played by the right stick of a right-handed drummer. Changing between ride and hi-hat, or between either and a leaner sound with neither, is often used to mark a change from one passage to another, for example to distinguish verse and chorus.

Ride cymbal - usually a heavy, standard cymbal, about 20" in diameter. It most often used to keep a constant rhythm similar function of hi-hats. Drummers change between hi-hat and ride cymbal to mark change in sections of songs, such as chorus and bridges.

Crash cymbal - makes the strongest accents, marking crescendos and climaxes, vocal entries, and major changes of mood. It is often accompanied by a strong kick on the bass drum pedal, both for musical effect and to support the stroke. There are many sizes of crash cymbals and the smaller the size, the higher sound it will produce.

Notation - A system of notes and symbols used to express musical ideas in a musical composition

Transcription / Sheet - The whole song piece for drums.

Drum Key / Sheet Legend - A map of what each note or symbol of transcription represents in a drum set.

Every drum transcription has its own drum key so you must learn it first before reading the whole sheet.

A drumstick is what you use to play the drums, cymbals and other percussive accesories. Wooden sticks are usually made from trees like Hickory, Oak, Maple and Hornbeam. There are also plastic and metal drumsticks.

Regardless of material, a drumstick has the following parts and elements:

Tip - key part of the drumstick as it touches your drums. It is usually made of wood or nylon and has different shapes like round, oval, tear drop, etc. The shape and material has a direct effect on your sound.

Shoulder - have a drastic impact on the feel of the stick. It greatly changes the weight distribution of the stick and the balance point of the stick.

Body / shaft - longest and biggest area of a drumstick. It is usually used to produce specialty strokes like the snare rim shots

Balance point / fulcrum - part of a drummer's grip that creates a stable point of contact with the stick and a pivot point such that the stick can rotate as a lever from that axis point

Grip area - thicker, counter balance end of a drumstick to hold it.

Butt - the other side of the tip. Drummers sometimes hold the stick in the shoulder and uses the butt-end for more powerful effect.

Diameter - How thick? The majority of sticks have a diameter of around 0.57" (1.45cm) but feel free to try thicker or thiner sticks that may match your feel. This also affects the overall weight of the stick.

Length - How long? The majority of sticks are around 16" (40.6cm) long. A longer drumstick will give you more reach but it will increase the overall weight of the stick.

One of the most important techniques a drummer must know is how to hold the drumsticks properly. When you play the drums with the correct stick grip, you will get the maximum bounce and control out of your drumsticks. You will be able to play the drums with much more efficiency and power with the proper grips.

There are different kinds of stick grips, but for today we will learn about the basics, which is the Matched Grip. To hold the stick with matched grip, follow the following steps:

  1. Grasp the stick between the fleshy part of the thumb and the first joint of the index finger about 5 inches from the butt end of the stick. This point of contact is called the fulcrum.
  2. Curve the tip of the index finger gently around the stick and wrap the remaining three fingers lightly around the stick
  3. Lift your forearm until it is parallel to the inclination of the drum part you want to hit and with the tip of the stick about few inches near the center. This is also applicable for hi-hat and ride cymbals. For crash cymbals, position your forearm slightly lower so that the neck will touch the edge of the cymbal.
  4. Make a 90 degree angle with your forearm by slightly moving your elbow away from your body. This is called the Geman Grip.
  5. With your wrist, lift up the drum stick about 45 degrees.
  6. Finally, hit the drum part by quickly turning your wrist down allowing the stick to fall freely.
