Music Theory

Session 1

A note is literally a pitched sound. It also refers to a sign or symbol used in musical notation to represent the relative duration and pitch of a sound.

We use letters A to G recursively to define the pitch of a note. A has lower pitch then B, G has higher pitch that F, and so on.

When a note is higher that G, we use letter A again and when a note is lower than A, we use letter G again.

This is an example of how notes are lined up:
A B C D E F G A B C D E F G A B C D E F G A B C D E F G

In between each note, there are also pitches that are either higher or lower – these are the sharps and flats.

A Sharp note, symbolized by #, has a pitch higher than the attached note but lower than the next note.
For example, A# (A Sharp) is higher than A but lower than B

Caution: There are two notes that does not have sharp counterparts - these are B and E. If in case you encountered a B# in a musical piece, it actually means C and E# actually means F.

This is an example of how sharp notes are lined up:
A - A# - B - C - C# - D - D# - E - F - F# - G - G# - A - A# - B - C - C# -

A Flat note, symbolized by b, has a pitch lower than the attached note but higher than the previous note.
For example, Bb (B Flat) is lower than B but higher than A

Caution: There are two notes that does not have flat counterparts – these are C and F. If in case you encountered a Cb in a musical piece, it actually means B. Fb actually means E.

This is an example of how flat notes are lined up:
Ab - A - Bb - B - C - Db - D - Eb - E - F - Gb - G - Ab - A - Bb - B - C - Db - D

In case you noticed, sharps and flats are related to each other. If fact, A sharp note is equivalent to a specific flat note.
For example, if A# > A and < B, and Bb < B and > A, then A# = Bb

Lastly, when a note is neither sharp nor flat, it is called a Neutral note.
A, B and C are neutral notes.

A Staff, or stave, is the foundation upon which notes are drawn. The modern staff comprises five lines and four spaces.
Every line and space on the staff represents a note.

A Clef assigns individual notes to lines and spaces.
The two most common clefs are Treble and Bass clefs.

The Treble clef, or G-Clef, is drawn on a staff where the center curve is at the second line.
It is also called G-clef because any note symble placed at the second line is assigned as G. Other note above the second line, including spaces, are higher than G, those placed below the second line are lower than G.

It is also called F-clef because any note symbol placed at the fourth line is assigned as F. Other note above the fourth line, including spaces, are higher than F, those placed below the fourth line are lower than F.

Session 2

Music Scale, or just scale, is a sequence of related notes of different pitches in an ascending or descending order.
A scale basically starts with a note called the Key.
The intervals between each note are determined by the type of scale.

Intervals in music means the distance between two notes. Below are the most common intervals used in music scales.

Semitone (aka. Half Step or Half Tone) is an interval between two adjacent notes without skipping any sharp or flat notes.
Please remember that C and E not have sharp notes, and C and F do not have flat notes.

Examples:
  • A to A#
  • B to C (B does not have sharp note)
  • E to F (E does not have sharp note)
  • C# to D
  • Bb to B

Tone (aka. Whole Step or Whole Tone) is an interval composed of two semitones.

Examples:
  • C to D (skipped C#)
  • F# to G# (skipped G)
  • B to C# (skipped C)
  • Eb to F (skppied E)

Octave is an interval of between two notes of the same letter. One octave is equivalent to 12 semitones. A scale starts with the key note and then ends with either higher or lower octave of that key note.

Examples:
  • A to higher A
  • F to lower F

When plotting the notes of a scale in music staff, we must first define the Key Signature of those notes.

A Key Signature is a series of sharp and flat symbols placed at the start of a staff in a music sheet that automatically defines all notes lying on a certain line or space as either sharp or flat notes.

This will avoid putting sharp or flat symbols next to a note symbol all the time.

In the absence of key signature, it means that there are neither sharps nor flats to be played in the music piece, unless you explicitly put or flat symbols next to the notes.

One good example of absence of key signature is in the C Major Scale> where every note is neutral.

A Scale Degree, also called Scale Step, is the name given to a particular note of a scale in relation to their position from the key note.

  1. Tonic
  2. Supertonic
  3. Mediant
  4. Subdominant
  5. Dominant
  6. Submediant
  7. Leading Tone
  8. Subtonic

For example, in C Major scale: C D E F G A B C,
since F is the 4th note, we can say that F is the Subdominant note of C Major scale,
A is the Leading Tone, and so on.

Solfa, aka. solfège (French) or solfeggio (Italian), is a music education method used to teach pitch and sight singing. It enables the musician to audiate, or mentally hear, the pitches of a piece of music which he or she is seeing for the first time and then to sing them aloud. It assigns the notes of a scale a particular syllable - DO RE MI FA SOL LA TI DO.

Please take note that Scale Degrees and Solfa Syllableare not bound to a single scale key, for example C. You can use them on any key.

A Chord in music is a combination of 3 or more notes played either simultaneously or in sequence. Chords are built off a single note called root.

The common type of chords are the Triads (meaning three), which are created with a root, third and fifth. The two most common triads are Major and Minor triads.

Major Triads

A major triad (or major chord), symbolized by M, maj or nothing, is built by adding the 3rd and 5th notes from the major scale of a root note being the 1st note.

In scale degrees, get the Tonic, Mediant and Dominant notes. In Solfa Syllables, get the Do, Mi and Sol notes.

For example: To get C Major Triad (or just C), get its major scale which is:
C – D – E – F – G – A – B – C
Then get the 1st, 3rd and 5th notes (or the Do-Mi-Sol). Therefore, C Major chord is composed of C – E – G

Minor Triads

A minor triad (or minor chord), symbolized by m or min, is built by adding the 3rd and 5th notes from the minor scale of a root note being the 1st note.

In scale degrees, get the Submediants, Tonic and Mediant notes. In Solfa Syllables, get the La, Do and Mi notes.

For example: To get C Minor Triad (or just Cm), get its minor scale which is:
C – D – Eb – F – G – Ab – Bb – C
Then get the 1st, 3rd and 5th notes (or the La-Do-Mi). Therefore, C Minor chord is composed of C – Eb – G

Chord Inversion is a method of rearranging the notes of a chord to produce a better melodic sound relative to the musical piece or song being played.

  • Root Position - The normal position of a chord.
    1 – 3 – 5
    Ex. C – E – G
  • 1st Inversion - This places the root note (1) to the top making it:
    3 – 5 – 1
    Ex. E – G – C
  • 2nd Inversion - This places the 5th note to the bottom making it:
    5 – 1 – 3
    Ex. G – C – E

Session 3

Rhythm refers to the element of time in music.
This includes everything from beats, measures, the groupings of notes into beats, to counting, to tempo, and the movement from one note to the next.

Some important larger ideas connected to rhythm are time signatures, tempo, and note values.

In musical terminology, Tempo (Italian for time, plural: tempi) is the speed or pace of a given piece. It is a crucial element of any musical composition, as it can affect the mood and difficulty of a piece.

The tempo of a piece will typically be written at the start of a piece of music usually indicated in Beats Per Minute (BPM). This means that a particular note value (for example, a quarter note) is specified as the beat, and the marking indicates that a certain number of these beats must be played per minute.

Tempo can be named as the following depending on the ranges of its BPM.
Below are some of familiar names:

  • Larghissimo – very, very slow (20 BPM and below)
  • Adagio – slow and stately (literally, "at ease") (51–60 BPM)
  • Moderato – moderately (81–90 BPM)
  • Allegro – fast, quickly and bright (105–132 BPM)
  • Presto – very fast (168–177 BPM)
  • Prestissimo – extremely fast (178–208 BPM)

In music notation, a note value, or note duration, indicates the relative duration of a note, using the color or shape of the note head, the presence or absence of a stem, and the presence or absence of flags, beams, hooks or tails.

A Rest in music indicates a silence of an equivalent duration.

Tie is a curved line that links two notes of the same pitch to show that their durations are to be added together. Do not be confused Tie with Slur, which is another curved line used to indicate that notes should be played smoothly and in one breath.

A note value may be augmented by adding a dot after it. This dot adds the next lower note value, making it one and a half times its original duration.

The Time Signature (also known as meter signature) is a notational convention used in Western musical notation to specify how many beats are in each measure/bar and which note value constitutes one beat.

In a musical score, the time signature appears at the beginning of the piece, as a time symbol or stacked numerals (such as 3/4) immediately following the key signature (or immediately following the clef if the key signature is empty). A mid-score time signature, usually immediately following a barline, indicates a change of meter.

Simple time signatures consist of two numerals, one stacked above the other:

The lower numeral indicates the note value which represents one beat (the beat unit). The upper numeral indicates how many such beats there are in a bar.

For instance, 2/4 means two quarter-note (crotchet) beats per bar; 3/8 means three eighth-note (quaver) beats per bar.

The most common simple time signatures are 2/4, 3/4, and 4/4.

A Semicircle, or C symbol, is sometimes used for 4/4 time, also called common time or imperfect time.

The symbol is derived from a broken circle used in music notation from the 14th through 16th centuries, where a full circle represented what today would be written in 3/2 or 3/4 time, and was called perfect time.

Session 4

A music genre is a conventional category that identifies pieces of music as belonging to a shared tradition or set of conventions. It may be defined by the musical techniques, the styles, the context and content and spirit of the themes.

The common basis for categorizing music are:

  • Geographical origin – “Pinoy Rock” vs “Chicago Blues”
  • Popular and traditional –“Pop” vs “Country”
  • Time period – “New Age” vs “Classical”
  • Techniques used – “Funk” vs “Jazz”

Music genres can also have sub-genres which arise by the development of new forms and styles of music and also simply by creating a new categorization.

For a complete list of all popular music genres, go to this Wikipedia link. For now, we will discuss some of the fundamental music genres that influences the modern music genres we listen to today.

Rock music is a genre of popular music that originated as rock and roll in the United States in the 1950s, and developed into a range of different styles in the 1960s and later, particularly in the United Kingdom and the United States. It has its roots in 1940s' and 1950s' rock and roll, itself heavily influenced by rhythm and blues and country music.

Typically, rock is song-based music usually with a 4/4 time signature using a verse-chorus form.

The sound of rock is traditionally centered on the electric guitar, supported by an electric bass guitar and percussion produced from a drum kit that combines drums and cymbals. This trio of instruments has often been complemented by the inclusion of others, particularly keyboards such as the piano, Hammond organ and synthesizers.

Rock places a higher degree of emphasis on musicianship, live performance, and an ideology of authenticity than pop music.

Rock music produced other sub-genres such as Pop Rock, Heavy Metal, Progressive Rock, Punk Rock, etc. Among the popular artists under the rock genre are Elvis Presley, Beatles, Metalica, Dreamtheater and Rivermaya.

Pop music (a term that originally derives from an abbreviation of popular) is a genre of popular music, which originated in its modern form in the 1950s, deriving from rock and roll.

Unlike rock, pop music focuses on vocals, melodies and lyrics. It emphasizes how a song was composed rather than how the musicians played it.

Pop music also appeals to the general audience, rather than a particular culture. Pop songs tend to reflect existing trends to relate to people. Pop music encourages dancing with its dance-oriented rhythms.

Pop music evolved into sub-genres including K-pop, Electropop, Latin Ballad, etc. Some of the popular artists under pop genre are Britney Spears, Michael Jackson, Katy Perry, Justin Beiber, etc.

Blues genre originated in African-American communities in the "Deep South" of the United States around the end of the 19th century. The genre is a fusion of traditional African music and European folk music, spirituals, work songs, field hollers, shouts and chants, and rhymed simple narrative ballads.

The music of the blues uses the Blues scale (will discuss in Advanced level) as foundation and employs a 12- bar song structure. The lyrics of early traditional blues verses consisted of a single line repeated four times.

Some of the sub-genres of blues music are Blues rock, Piano blues, Rhythm and blues, etc. Some of the popular blues artists are Muddy Waters, BB King, Elvis Presley, Eric Clapton, Juan Dela Cruz Band, etc.

Country music is a genre of American popular music that originated in Southern United States, in Atlanta, Georgia in the 1920s. It takes its roots from the southeastern genre of American folk music and Western music.

Country music often consists of ballads and dance tunes with generally simple forms and harmonies accompanied by mostly string instruments such as banjos, electric and acoustic guitars, fiddles, and harmonicas.

Some of the sub-genres of country music are Bluegrass, Country rock, Americana, Honky Tonk, etc. Some of the popular artists under country music are John Denver, Dan Fogelberg, Don Moen, etc.

Jazz originated in the late 19th to early 20th century as interpretations of American and European classical music entwined with African and slave folk songs and the influences of West African culture.

Jazz makes heavy use of improvisation, polyrhythms, syncopation, and the swung note, as well as aspects of European harmony, American popular music, the brass band tradition, and African musical elements such as blue notes and ragtime.

Among the popular jazz artists are Louis Armstrong, Norah Jones, Michael Buble, Dave Coz, Apo Hiking Society, etc.
